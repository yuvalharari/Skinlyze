Types of Skin Lesions

The following lesion types are recognized and analyzed by the Skinlyze model, trained on the HAM10000 dataset. These descriptions are provided for educational purposes and reflect common clinical characteristics.

Melanoma (MEL)

The most dangerous form of skin cancer. Originates from melanocytes and may present as an asymmetrical lesion with irregular borders, multiple colors, and changes over time (evolution). Early detection is critical for survival.

Suspicious

Basal Cell Carcinoma (BCC)

The most common type of skin cancer. Typically slow-growing and rarely metastasizes, but may cause significant local damage if untreated. Often appears as a pearly bump, translucent lesion, or may ulcerate and bleed.

Suspicious

Actinic Keratosis (AKIEC)

A rough, scaly patch caused by long-term ultraviolet (UV) exposure. Considered a pre-cancerous lesion with potential progression to squamous cell carcinoma if left untreated.

Suspicious

Melanocytic Nevi (NV)

Common moles resulting from a benign proliferation of melanocytes. Usually harmless and symmetric in appearance, but any changes in size, shape, or color should be evaluated by a dermatologist.

Not Suspicious

Benign Keratosis (BKL)

A group of non-cancerous skin growths including seborrheic keratosis and solar lentigo. These lesions are generally benign and do not require treatment unless for cosmetic reasons or if symptomatic.

Not Suspicious

Dermatofibroma (DF)

A common benign skin lesion, often found on the legs. Typically firm to the touch and may exhibit a characteristic "dimple sign" when pinched. Usually harmless and requires no treatment.

Not Suspicious

Vascular Lesions (VASC)

Lesions originating from blood vessels, including cherry angiomas and other vascular abnormalities. These are generally benign and not associated with skin cancer.

Not Suspicious

The ABCDE Rule

A simple guide used by dermatologists to identify potential warning signs of melanoma.

A

Asymmetry

One half of the mole does not match the other. Benign moles are usually symmetrical.

B

Border

Irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred borders are warning signs. Benign moles have smooth, even borders.

C

Color

Variation in color - shades of brown, black, red, white, or blue - can indicate melanoma.

D

Diameter

Lesions larger than 6mm (about the size of a pencil eraser) warrant attention, though smaller ones can also be dangerous.

E

Evolution

Any change in size, shape, color, or symptoms such as bleeding or itching over time should be evaluated by a dermatologist.
